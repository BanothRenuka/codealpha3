Pharmacovigilance & Adverse Drug Reaction (ADR) Analysis

Drug: Paracetamol (Acetaminophen)

1. Introduction

Pharmacovigilance is the science concerned with the detection, assessment, understanding, and prevention of adverse effects or any other drug-related problems. Paracetamol (Acetaminophen) is one of the most widely used analgesic and antipyretic drugs worldwide. Despite its general safety at therapeutic doses, paracetamol is a leading cause of drug-induced liver injury when overdosed.

This report analyzes the adverse drug reactions (ADRs) associated with paracetamol, its mechanism of action, and measures to minimize associated risks, based on data reported in WHO VigiBase and DrugBank.

2. Mechanism of Action

Paracetamol acts primarily in the central nervous system. It inhibits the cyclooxygenase (COX) enzyme, particularly COX-2, reducing the synthesis of prostaglandins involved in pain and fever.

Unlike NSAIDs, paracetamol has minimal peripheral anti-inflammatory effects. It is believed to modulate serotonergic pathways and may also influence endocannabinoid systems contributing to analgesia.

Paracetamol is metabolized in the liver. A small fraction is converted to a toxic metabolite, N-acetyl-p-benzoquinone imine (NAPQI), which is detoxified by glutathione under normal conditions. In overdose, glutathione is depleted, leading to hepatocellular damage.

3. Reported Adverse Drug Reactions (ADRs)

Data Sources: WHO VigiBase, DrugBank

System Organ Class Adverse Drug Reactions
Gastrointestinal Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain
Hepatobiliary Elevated liver enzymes, hepatitis, acute liver failure
Skin Rash, urticaria, Stevens-Johnson syndrome (rare)
Immune System Hypersensitivity reactions, anaphylaxis (rare)
Renal Acute kidney injury (with chronic high-dose use)

The most serious ADR associated with paracetamol is dose-dependent hepatotoxicity. WHO pharmacovigilance reports highlight overdose as a major cause of acute liver failure, particularly in adults with chronic alcohol use or malnutrition.

4. Measures to Reduce Risks

  • Adherence to recommended therapeutic doses (maximum 4 g/day in adults).
  • Avoiding concurrent use of multiple paracetamol-containing products.
  • Caution in patients with liver disease, alcoholism, or malnutrition.
  • Public education on overdose risks and safe use.
  • Early administration of N-acetylcysteine (NAC) in cases of overdose.
  • Clear labeling and pharmacovigilance reporting of suspected ADRs.

5. Conclusion

Paracetamol remains an effective and widely used analgesic and antipyretic drug. However, its potential for severe hepatotoxicity necessitates careful monitoring, patient education, and active pharmacovigilance.

Continuous ADR reporting to databases such as WHO VigiBase plays a crucial role in identifying safety signals and improving drug use practices worldwide.
